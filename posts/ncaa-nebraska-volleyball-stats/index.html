The 7th Player: How the Devaney Center Forces Opponent Errors

ncaa
volleyball
huskers
Author

Susie Kastl

Published

April 10, 2026

Is the Devaney Center Effect Real? If you’ve been to the Devaney Center for a match, you know it’s more than just a packed house. The sellout streak is legendary, but the real story is the constant noise that hits visiting teams the moment they take the court. It’s an environment designed to be overwhelming, forcing opponents to face both a top-tier roster and a relentless atmosphere.

The Huskers haven’t lost a regular-season home game in years, but is that just because they are talented, or does the crowd noise actually force opponent errors? By looking at “service errors” and “attack errors” for visiting teams compared to when those teams play elsewhere, the data suggests the Lincoln atmosphere rattles the best players in the country. It’s a home-court advantage that goes far beyond the points on the scoreboard.

The Chaos Factor

To understand the “Devaney Effect,” we first have to look at how Big Ten opponents handle the pressure in Lincoln compared to the rest of the conference. If the crowd is truly a “7th player,” we should see it reflected in the frequency of unforced errors.

When we break down opponent errors per set across the Big Ten, the numbers tell a clear story. Nebraska leads the pack in forcing mistakes, proving that playing in front of the Red Sea is a fundamentally different experience than playing anywhere else in the country.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(ggrepel)

opp_stats <- read_csv("ncaaopponent25.csv") |> 
  filter(Conference == "Big Ten") |>
  mutate(Errors_Per_Set = `Opp Errors` / S)

ggplot() +
  geom_bar(data = opp_stats, aes(x = reorder(Team, Errors_Per_Set), weight = Errors_Per_Set), fill = "grey") +
  geom_bar(data = opp_stats |> filter(Team == "Nebraska"), aes(x = Team, weight = Errors_Per_Set), fill = "#E41C38") +
  coord_flip() +
  theme_minimal() + 

  labs(
    title = "Opponent Performance at the Devaney Center",
    subtitle = "Nebraska leads the Big Ten in forcing opponent errors per set, proving the 'Devaney Effect' is real.",
    x = "Big Ten Team",
    y = "Opponent Errors Per Set",
    caption = "Source: NCAA Statistics | By Susie Kastl"
  ) +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(face = "bold", size = 16),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size = 10),
    axis.title = element_text(size = 9)
  )

A Consistent Fortress

Consistency is the hallmark of the Husker fan base. While other programs might see their attendance spike or dip based on their record or the quality of their opponents, Nebraska has turned the Devaney Center into a permanent fixture at the top of the rankings.

By tracking the conference attendance leaders over the last four seasons, we can see just how unshakable this home-court advantage has become. While other teams “bump” up and down the rankings, Nebraska’s spot at the top is a constant.

Does Noise Equal Mistakes?

The final piece of the puzzle is the direct relationship between the size of the crowd and the mistakes made by visitors. Does a bigger crowd actually lead to more chaos?

By plotting average attendance against total opponent errors, we can see if there is a statistical “breaking point” where the noise becomes too much. The trend is hard to ignore: as the seats fill up, the opponent’s ability to play clean volleyball begins to break down.

Code 
attendance <- read_csv("ncaaattendance25.csv") |> select(-Rank)
opponent_hitting <- read_csv("ncaaopponent25.csv") |> 
  select(-Rank) |> 
  rename(Institution = Team)
devaney <- attendance |> inner_join(opponent_hitting)

ggplot() + 
  geom_point(data=devaney, aes(x=`Avg Attendance`, y=`Opp Errors`), color="grey", alpha=.5) +
  geom_smooth(data=devaney, aes(x=`Avg Attendance`, y=`Opp Errors`), method="lm", se=FALSE, color="black") +
  geom_point(data=devaney |> filter(Institution == "Nebraska"), 
             aes(x=`Avg Attendance`, y=`Opp Errors`), color="#E41C38", size=4) +
  geom_text_repel(data=devaney |> filter(Institution == "Nebraska"), 
                  aes(x=`Avg Attendance`, y=`Opp Errors`, label=Institution)) +
  
  labs(
    title="Does the Crowd Cause Chaos?", 
    subtitle="The relationship between home-court attendance and opponent hitting mistakes.", 
    x="Average Attendance", 
    y="Opponent Errors",
    caption="Source: NCAA Statistics | By Susie Kastl"
  ) +
  theme_minimal() +
theme(
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
    plot.title = element_text(face = "bold", size = 16),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size = 10),
    axis.title = element_text(size = 9)
  )

The Verdict

So, is the Devaney Center truly a 7th player for Nebraska? To answer your question, yes.

While the Huskers’ elite recruiting and coaching are the foundation of their success, the atmosphere in Lincoln acts as a force multiplier. Opponents don’t just lose to a better team; they lose to an environment that forces more mental mistakes than any other venue in the Big Ten. Nebraska Volleyball is a powerhouse, but with over 8,000 screaming fans backing them up, they aren’t just playing a different game—they’re forcing their opponents to play a worse one.

The Devaney Effect is real, and it’s a major reason why the road to a national championship consistently runs through Lincoln.
